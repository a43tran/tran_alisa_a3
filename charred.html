Charred

A hot-pot sculpture composed of air-dry clay with acrylic paint and wire supports.

Eating hot pot is a communal experience, involving each guest at the dinner table in preparing ingredients and sharing cooked toppings. The dinner table becomes a place of fulfillment, laughter and kinship. However, many refuse to express their negative emotions for fear of disrupting the positive environment and relationships that have been established around this dinner table.

Hence, Charred challenges the self-damaging belief that suppressing our inner conflicts, negative emotions, concerns, and stresses, prevents external conflicts with others. The stainless steel hot pot container contains 14 different ingredients moulded into tangible manifestations of the worries individuals have been brewing within themselves and are unspoken at the dinner table. The sculpture is interactive, inviting viewers to observe and touch each ingredient from the pot onto their personal “plates”.

Air-dry clay sculpture with wire supports & acrylic paint, 2024

The Ingredients

Despite the communal experience hotpot brings, I wanted to design a sculpture that contradicts this seemingly perfect scene, unveiling what is hidden underneath through these 14 different ingredients.

Through the principle of interactivity, viewers can pick up ingredients from the pot and place them onto their plates as if eating the meal themselves.

Opening the lid and removing the ingredients from the pot represents the act of releasing the negative emotions we have deflected for so long, prompting individuals to embrace these authentic emotions as a natural part of the human experience.

Artistic Influence

Stephanie H Shih

During research, I discovered Stephanie H Shih, a Taiwanese-American ceramic artist, who meticulously recreates ordinary, traditional and widely popular Asian snacks and foods in clay, such as White Rabbit Candy, Spam, and Asian vinegar and condiments (Atkins, 2023). These sculptures serve as a profound way for her to explore the intersections between different cultures and her cultural identity as someone born in the US but of Taiwanese descent. What stood out to me was the intricate modelling and painting that not only realistically mirrors each food’s structure and composition, but also evokes the meaningful emotions and memories familiarly associated with cooking, eating and sharing such foods in the warmth of one’s home.

Emotional Detox, Marc Quinn

Another influence was Marc Quinn’s series of sculptures titled “Emotional Detox”. These facial sculptures were composed of lead and were casted directly from Quinn’s body. Inspired by the seven deadly sins, each sculpture displays the aching release of suppressed emotions as if the sculpted individual had newly “experienced, forced out and sweated [out these heavy emotions] through [their] skin (Tate Britain Exhibition, 2022). I admired Quinn’s expressive and direct interpretation of the implications that emerge from burying sorrow.
