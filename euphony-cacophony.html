Euphony & Cacophony

An acrylic painting depicting a fractured story of silence.

Euphony & Cacophony depicts a dimension where silence is the new “talking”, with objects that represent the different types of silences in our mundane lives.

The snapped violin represents a frustrated musician failing to hit a high note. Silence can feel tense, as we pressure ourselves to be the best performer we can be. From its unrepairable state, the violin reflects a musician’s internal turmoil.

The torn cherries and seeds demonstrate how in the face of justice, remaining neutral is poisonous, though sweet at first bite. To remain silent amid injustices is complicity with the oppressor; an act of condoning and normalizing these harmful actions.

The lighthouse serves as one’s unspoken longing for a companion amidst the darkness. Lighthouses are tall beacons that guide many sailors during dark storms, yet the structure remains isolated on its own platform.

The Spix's macaw, a once-extinct bird, flies with a newfound sense of freedom and release, yet is juxtaposed by the suffocating overlap of field bind weeds. By bottling our emotions, they can uncontrollably accumulate and disrupt our mental well-being and relationships, likewise, an untreated yard of field bindweed can tightly choke other plants and monopolize the soil’s nutrients.

11” x 14”, Acrylic on wood, 2024

The Process

Mind Map

In the design process, I gathered various source images on the internet to determine my theme. I landed on three main ideas, such as the power of silence in our lives, order and chaos between the city and nature, and friendship and loneliness regarding emotional and physical presence. I narrowed it down to the topic of silence, given my interest in its core definition.

Artistic Influence

In Silence, Chiharu Shiota

Some artistic influences include Chiharu Shiota. Upon witnessing her neighbour’s house burnt down, Shiota weaved miles of thread in, around and through the charred piano and chairs in the installation, mimicking the destructive and overwhelming nature of the flames that consumed her neighbour’s grand piano. Most people associate silence with emptiness, but Shiota contrasts this perception through the mass of black thread, evoking noises of death, grief, loss and desolation.

4'33", John Cage

I was also influenced by John Cage, a musician who composed a 4 minute and 33 second song of silence. During the silent performance, the attention was shifted from the John Cage to the audience who coughed and shifted in their seats. This piece prompted me to reflect on the abstract idea of how there might not be any such thing as silence.
